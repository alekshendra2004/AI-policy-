Age & timing verdict

Giving this exam at 23 is completely normal — right in the middle of the typical range. UPSC general goes to 32. Think tanks, fellowships, and PhD admissions have no age sensitivity at this stage. The only number that matters is the CUET PG cutoff. A 23-year-old entering JNU with Google Analytics done, a language started, and a clear research direction is ahead of most 21-year-olds who showed up straight from BA.

Exam day — 3-pass strategy (90 min, 75 questions)
0–25 min
Pass 1 — Certainties only. Attempt only questions you are 100% sure about. Skip anything with doubt. Target: 35–40 answered. Pace: ~40 sec/question.
25–55 min
Pass 2 — Can eliminate 2 options. Return to skipped questions. Attempt if you can eliminate at least 2 options. If 50/50 between 2 — skip.
55–80 min
Pass 3 — 3 options eliminated. Only attempt if 3 options are clearly wrong. Pure guess (1-in-4) = skip. Negative marking is real.
80–90 min
Final review. Don't change answers unless 100% certain it's wrong. First instinct is usually correct. Submit with confidence. Start with Unit IV questions.

General Reading List

Not exam targets. Books that build the mind behind the career. Read 30 min/day alongside exam prep — mornings or before sleep.

The sequencing logic: IR comes first — always. The books below are not competitors to exam prep. They run in the background at 30 min/day, one book active at a time. The order moves from thinking infrastructure → analytical frameworks → IR-specific depth → writing craft → AI governance (deferred until JNU years). Books marked defer are for the post-JNU phase when AI governance becomes the active focus.

Currently reading
Reading now · Book 01
The Art of Thinking Clearly
Rolf Dobelli
Active

99 cognitive biases explained in 2–3 pages each. The most important book for anyone who will spend a career making arguments — in research, in policy briefs, in product decisions. Confirmation bias, sunk cost fallacy, survivorship bias — he names every trap that will distort IR analysis if unexamined. Read 2–3 chapters daily. No heavy notes needed — just recognition. After finishing, write down your top 5 recurring thinking errors and keep that list permanently.

3–4 weeks 2–3 chapters/day Thinking infrastructure
Phase 1 — Thinking & writing infrastructure · Read before or alongside NCERTs
Book 02 · After Art of Thinking Clearly
Several Short Sentences About Writing
Verlyn Klinkenborg
Do next

The most immediately practical writing book that exists. Klinkenborg's method — short sentences, one idea per sentence, no sentence that cannot be defended — is the foundation of every policy brief, op-ed, and research paper he will write. The exam thinker comparison tables, the JNU essay answers, the ORF commentary — all of them get better after this book. Read slowly, do every exercise. This is a workbook, not a reading book.

2–3 weeksDo every exerciseWriting craft
Book 03 · During Pavneet Singh phase
Thinking Fast and Slow
Daniel Kahneman
Phase 2

System 1 and System 2 thinking is the framework every policymaker, researcher, and analyst references. For IR specifically — understanding how cognitive shortcuts distort foreign policy decisions, how negotiators make errors under pressure, how public opinion on international events is shaped by availability bias — this book is the analytical backbone. Read heavily annotated. Slow book. Do not rush it.

6–8 weeksHeavy annotationAnalytical framework
Phase 2 — Analytical architecture · Read during Gauba & thinkers phase (Jul–Oct)
Book 04 · September–October
Seeing Like a State
James C. Scott
High priority

One of the most important books on this entire list for IR. Scott's central argument — that states impose legibility on complex social systems and fail catastrophically because local knowledge cannot be standardised — is directly relevant to state theory, development policy, IR institutional failure, and later AI governance. Every JNU thesis that engages with state capacity will benefit from this framework. Read slowly, annotate every chapter with one IR application. You will cite this for a decade.

6–8 weeksAnnotate heavilyState theory · IR core
Book 05 · October–November
The Tragedy of Great Power Politics
John J. Mearsheimer
IR essential

Offensive realism — the idea that great powers are structurally condemned to compete for power regardless of intentions. Read Chapters 1–3 carefully for the structural realism framework. This is Unit IV exam content made rigorous. For JNU IR, Mearsheimer is a foundational thinker — professors will expect familiarity. The structural realism lens also explains India-China competition, US-China rivalry, and NATO expansion in ways that surface-level news coverage cannot. Read Part 1 carefully, use rest as reference.

5–6 weeksCh.1–3 carefullyRealism · Exam relevant
Book 06 · November–December
Superforecasting
Philip Tetlock
Analytical edge

Teaches calibrated probabilistic reasoning — the skill that separates good analysts from opinion-givers. Tetlock's research shows that specific thinking habits produce dramatically better predictions about geopolitical events. For IR research and think tank work, the ability to express calibrated uncertainty rather than false confidence is a core professional competency. After reading, you will express uncertainty in ranges rather than certainties. Your analysis becomes more honest and more useful.

4–5 weeksTest yourself on exercisesForecasting · Analysis
Phase 3 — Domain depth · Read during JNU Semesters 1–2 (2027–2028)
Book 07 · JNU Semester 1
Why Nations Fail
Daron Acemoglu & James Robinson
JNU Sem 1

Institutions as the primary driver of development and state outcomes — extractive vs inclusive institutions determine whether nations prosper. This framework maps directly onto IR: why some states become regional powers while others remain peripheral, how colonial institutional legacies shape foreign policy, why development aid fails in weak institutional contexts. Book 08 · JNU Semester 1–2
Poor Economics
Abhijit Banerjee & Esther Duflo
JNU Sem 1–2

Read for two things simultaneously: the content (how development interventions actually work or fail in poor communities, RCT methodology, evidence-based policy) and the craft (how to write rigorous analytical work accessibly without dumbing it down). Banerjee and Duflo are writing models as much as content models. For JNU IR students interested in development + IR intersection, this is the empirical backbone of South-South cooperation, foreign aid effectiveness, and India's development diplomacy research.

Book 09 · JNU Semester 2
Factfulness
Hans Rosling
JNU Sem 2

Data-driven correction of every instinct about global development. Rosling demonstrates that most people's mental models of the world are factually wrong in systematic ways — and for IR researchers writing about the Global South, operating from accurate baseline data is not optional. Also study Rosling's communication method: he is the best model for making data emotionally compelling without distorting it. Fast read but verify every instinct you have about development data after.

Book 10 · JNU Semester 2–3
Good Strategy / Bad Strategy
Richard Rumelt
JNU Sem 2–3

The clearest definition of what strategy actually means: a diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent actions. Most government policy documents and think tank reports Rumelt would classify as bad strategy — lists of goals dressed up as plans. Reading this makes you immediately better at writing policy briefs and identifying weak thinking in documents you critique. Apply the kernel framework (diagnosis → guiding policy → actions) to every policy document you read from now on. The gaps you find are your articles.

Book 11 · JNU Semester 3 · After writing your first 2 pieces
On Writing Well
William Zinsser
Sem 3

Where Klinkenborg gives sentence-level craft, Zinsser gives structure, voice, and the discipline of revision for long-form non-fiction. Read this after you have written your first two published pieces — you will understand it differently having produced work than before. The revision methodology alone is worth the read: it teaches you to cut, not just add. Read it a second time six months later — it teaches different things at different stages of your writing life.

Three rules for reading that actually builds thinking
01

Read for argument, not information. Every book has one central claim. Find it in the first 50 pages. Everything else is evidence. Once you have the claim and the key evidence, you have the book.

02

Annotate or it did not happen. Three questions per chapter: What is the author claiming? What evidence supports it? Where do I disagree or see a gap? If you cannot answer these, reread that chapter.

03

Apply immediately. After each book write one paragraph connecting its core idea to IR, India's foreign policy, or the career direction. This is not optional. The application is where reading becomes thinking.
